### Line-by-line translation

**Milyen jármű a trojka?**
What kind of vehicle is a *troika*?

**Három lovas orosz faszán.**
A Russian one with three horses — *up his ass*.
*(Vulgar punchline; the obscenity is the joke.)*

---

### Humour & why it works

This is a **two-line pub joke / schoolyard riddle** built on:

* **Legitimate quiz setup**
  *trojka* = a real Russian three-horse carriage.
* **Expectation of a factual answer**
* **Sudden obscene derailment**
  The second line grafts a crude insult onto an otherwise correct description.

The laugh comes from:

* **register collision** (encyclopaedic → vulgar)
* **phonetic snap** (the obscene noun lands hard at the end)
* **national stereotype bait** (old-school, intentionally dumb)

It’s not clever-wordplay humour; it’s **blunt shock timing**.

---

### Linguistically interesting bits

* **milyen jármű** – “what kind of vehicle”; textbook question frame.
* **trojka** – loanword; culturally specific, instantly recognisable.
* **három lovas** – “three-horse”; correct modifier phrase.
* **orosz** – “Russian”; neutral adjective until the punch.
* **faszán** – vulgar adverbial form built from *fasz*; here used as an insult, not a manner adverb.

---

### Register & genre

* **Low-register riddle joke**
* Typical of:

  * locker-room humour
  * late-night TV banter
  * deliberately unsophisticated delivery
* Works best **spoken**, with a pause before the last word.

---

### Why **„három lovas orosz szán”** *would* be parseable

If the answer were:

> **háromlovas orosz szán / szánkó**

that would be:

* completely grammatical
* semantically correct
* and the **expected encyclopaedic answer**

Hungarian happily parses:

* **háromlovas** = three-horse (compound adjective)
* **orosz** = Russian (national adjective)
* **szán / szánkó** = sled

So yes:
the brain initially **expects a wooden sled / sleigh** interpretation.

---

### What actually happens in the joke

The joke exploits **incremental parsing**:

1. **három lovas**
   → modifier phrase, still neutral

2. **orosz**
   → narrows reference (troika confirmed)

3. **faszán**
   → forces **catastrophic reanalysis**

At that point:

* *faszán* cannot be a head noun like *szán*
* it also cannot be a manner adverb (*“nicely”*) in this context
* so the listener is forced into the obscene idiom reading

This is a classic case of **garden-path humour**:

> your parser commits early, then gets rug-pulled at the final word.

---

### Why this works especially well in Hungarian

* Hungarian allows **long pre-nominal modifier chains** without signalling the head early.
* The **head comes last**, so:

  * you *have* to wait for it
  * and when it’s obscene, the reanalysis is maximal.
* *szán* and *faszán* are:

  * phonotactically compatible
  * stress-compatible
  * both plausible final heads *until* the taboo semantics crash in.

That’s why the joke feels “clean” right up to the last syllable.
