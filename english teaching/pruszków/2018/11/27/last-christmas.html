Very

We most often see “very” as an adverb which intensifies an adjective: very dark, very wet, etc., but it can also be used as an adjective meaning “the same” or “identical”: he left that very night.

Once bitten, twice shy

This is a proverb that means that if you’ve been hurt by something once, you’ll be more careful the next time.

It’s an example of elliptical causation: if once bitten, then twice shy: that is, the causation (if… then…) is omitted: this kind of omission is known as an ellipsis, which is also the name given to the triple dots (…) used to show a piece of a quotation has been omitted.

Other examples of elliptical causation include “no pain, no gain”, “waste not, want not”, “nothing ventured, nothing gained”.
